Why It's Wild:

With just 23 people, there's already a 50% chance of a birthday match. With 70 people, it jumps to 99.9%! Your intuition says it should take way more people, right?

Contrary

The Birthday Paradox

A counterintuitive probability problem showing how quickly shared birthdays become likely in a group.

The Question

How many people do you need in a room before there's a better-than-even chance that two of them share the same birthday?

Most people guess somewhere around 183 (half of 365 days), but the real answer is surprisingly low: just 23 people gives you a 50% chance!

Why It Works

Key Insight: We're not asking if someone shares YOUR specific birthday. We're asking if ANY two people in the room share a birthday with each other.

The Math: With 23 people, you get 253 unique pairs of people. That's a lot of opportunities for a match! Each pair has a 1/365 chance of matching.

The Formula: We calculate the probability that NO ONE shares a birthday, then flip it. P(match) = 1 - (365/365 × 364/365 × 363/365 × ... × 343/365)

Mind-Blowing Facts:

  • • With 50 people: 97% chance of a match
  • • With 70 people: 99.9% virtually guaranteed
  • • With 367 people: 100% certain (Pigeonhole Principle)

Learn More

First discussed in probability theory, this paradox has real-world applications in cryptography, hash collisions, and data security.
